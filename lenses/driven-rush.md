# Driven — Rush

**Track 2 / 4:27 / Test for Echo, 1996**

---

## What this song feels like

Something heavy leaning forward. Not falling — pushing. The riff sits on a single string and repeats like a machine that doesn't know how to stop, and the whole track has the feeling of a body in motion that can't remember deciding to start. There's grit in it. Weight. But also something underneath that isn't quite exhaustion — more like the refusal to feel exhaustion yet.

## Core emotional movement

**Pressure → Build → Loop**

The verse crouches low on that grinding single-string riff, pulling between the third, fifth, and sixth fret like a pendulum that keeps narrowing its swing. Then the pre-chorus opens into power chords — F, G, A minor — and the space widens just enough to breathe. But it never stays open. It pulls back into the riff. Back into the pressure. The chorus lifts but doesn't escape. The whole track cycles through this: compress, open, compress again. It doesn't end — it just keeps riding.

## What it's really about (emotionally)

The state of being propelled by something you can't name and can't put down. Not ambition — ambition has a target. This is closer to compulsion. The internal engine that keeps you moving past the point where moving makes sense. And the small, quiet question underneath it all: is this drive, or is this just the inability to stop? Peart put it plainly — "driven to the edge of a deep dark hole." The song lives right at that edge. Not falling in. Not stepping back. Just riding along it.

## How the music mirrors it

The track was built entirely from Lee's bass, and you can feel the bottom-heaviness of that origin — everything grows upward from a low center of gravity. Peart deliberately scaled back his drumming to give the song a heavier, blunter character, and it works. The drums don't dazzle. They drive. Lifeson's guitar is grungy, chaotic in the solo sections — harmonics, dive bombs, sustain that bleeds into noise. The production is thick and mid-'90s muscular, stripped of the synth layers that defined the previous decade. This is Rush standing in a room with their instruments and pushing air. Lee called it "a quintessential Rush song," and the reason is simple: it sounds exactly like what it's about — three people who can't stop moving forward.

## Emotional center

The weight of momentum that has outlived its reason.

## One clean sentence

The sound of someone who stopped choosing to move a long time ago and just hasn't figured out how to be still.
