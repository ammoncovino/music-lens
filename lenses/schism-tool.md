# Schism — Tool

**Track 5 / 6:47 / Lateralus, 2001**

---

## What this song feels like

Something that was once whole, split down the middle and still twitching. The bass riff enters like a heartbeat with a limp — five beats, then seven, five then seven — and your body tries to find the center of it and can't. The ground is always a half-step away from where you expect it. Nothing settles. Nothing lines up. And the feeling isn't chaos. It's the specific discomfort of two things that should fit together and don't anymore.

## Core emotional movement

**Loop → Pressure → Build → Break → Loop**

The song begins in the loop and never fully escapes it. The alternating 5/8 and 7/8 bars create a cycle that your body keeps trying to resolve into something even, something symmetrical — and it refuses. The pressure accumulates through the verses as Maynard's voice sits low and measured against the off-kilter pulse. The middle section shifts — 6/8 bars, then 3/8, then 3/4, the meter changing 47 times across the track — and the density builds until the guitars break open into heavy, distorted chords. For a moment, it's catharsis. Then it falls back into the riff. Back into the loop. The pieces almost come together. Almost. The song ends where it started: two halves that know they belong to each other and can't close the gap.

## What it's really about (emotionally)

The state of being close enough to feel the shape of what's missing. Not the grief of total loss — something more precise. The experience of two things that share the same center but can't occupy the same space. You can feel where the fit used to be. You can feel exactly how it broke. And the worst part isn't the distance. It's the almost. The repeated reaching and missing by the smallest margin. The emotional state is the gap itself — not one side or the other, but the space between, charged and empty at the same time.

## How the music mirrors it

The bass riff is the entire architecture. Justin Chancellor plays a pattern that divides into 5 and 7 — two odd numbers that add up to 12, which should feel even but doesn't, because the division is asymmetric. Your ear hears one long phrase; your body feels two unequal halves. That's the schism, built into the rhythm itself. Danny Carey's drums lock into the asymmetry with a hypnotic precision — kick drum landing on the downbeats of each odd grouping, snare off-center, hi-hat running eighth notes except where 16th-note triplets flare up like small disruptions in an otherwise controlled surface. Adam Jones's guitar enters palm-muted, percussive, more texture than melody — dead notes and hammer-ons that add grit without adding resolution. The vocal harmonies sit a semitone apart — close enough to rub, not close enough to resolve — and the dissonance mirrors the lyric: two things pressed together that can't fuse. The middle section's constant meter shifts — 6/8 to 3/8 to 3/4 to 9/8 to 10/8 — don't feel like showing off. They feel like the ground under you keeps fracturing, each new time signature a new crack in the floor. When the heavy distorted section finally arrives, the guitars open into something massive, and for a few bars the band locks into something that feels unified. Then it fragments again. The song won a Grammy for Best Metal Performance, but the real achievement is structural: it built the feeling of disconnection into the math itself.

## Emotional center

The space between two halves that remember being whole.

## One clean sentence

The sound of two things reaching for each other across a fracture that widens exactly as fast as they move.
