# La Villa Strangiato — Rush

**Track 4 / 9:35 / Hemispheres, 1978**

*Subtitled: "An Exercise in Self-Indulgence"*

---

## What this song feels like

Falling asleep in a room you trust and waking up in twelve places you don't. The classical guitar opening is intimate — warm hands, close air, the feeling of being eased downward into something soft. Then the dream takes over, and from that point forward you're not steering. You're being carried through rooms that keep changing shape, and the feeling is never fear exactly — it's the particular alertness of being inside something vivid that you can't control.

## Core emotional movement

**Stillness → Build → Drift → Impulse → Loop → Return**

The piece doesn't have one arc. It has the arc of a dream — which is to say, it shifts without warning and somehow still feels connected. The opening nylon guitar is pure stillness. "To Sleep, Perchance to Dream" builds a single chord into a gathering of textures, layering instruments like a slow tide rising under a still surface. Then the Strangiato theme drops you into something angular and disorienting — the harmonic language deliberately unsettled, the melody landing in places the voice can't comfortably follow. "A Lerxst in Wonderland" suspends you — weightless, floating, the time signature shaved just enough to make gravity feel optional. Then the Monsters hit, and the energy shifts to raw impulse — playful, aggressive, almost cartoonish in its attack. From there it cycles: bass solo, drum solo, guitar solo, each instrument grabbing you by the shoulder and turning you to face a new direction. The waltz section is tongue-in-cheek, lopsided, before the monsters return. Then the Strangiato theme reprises, and the final fifteen seconds — "A Farewell to Things" — close the door quietly behind you.

## What it's really about (emotionally)

The experience of being inside a mind that won't sit still. Not chaos — something more structured than chaos but less stable than waking life. The emotional state is lucid dreaming: you're aware enough to notice each shift, but you can't slow it down or choose what comes next. There's wonder in it. There's unease. There's a specific kind of joy that only exists when three people are playing at the absolute edge of what they can do and choosing to stay there for nine and a half minutes. Underneath all twelve movements, the real feeling is trust — the trust it takes to follow something that doesn't explain where it's going.

## How the music mirrors it

The nylon-string opening is Spanish classical — Lifeson on his Gibson acoustic, played with the intimacy of a room with one person in it. The "To Sleep" section builds on a single C major chord with an added D, layering instruments one by one without any harmonic movement — the stasis itself creates the dreamstate. When the Strangiato theme arrives, the harmony jumps between C major and F-sharp major — a tritone, the diabolus in musica — and the melody is deliberately angular, uncomfortable to sing, landing in places that feel like wrong turns in familiar hallways. "A Lerxst in Wonderland" shifts from 4/4 to 7/8, shaving a fraction off each rhythmic cycle so the ground never quite settles under you — Lifeson's guitar here sounds like a Romanian pan pipe, airy and suspended. The Monsters theme is borrowed from Raymond Scott's "Powerhouse" — a 1930s piece famous from Warner Brothers cartoons — which gives the section its playful menace. Geddy's bass goes wild in "Ghost of the Aragon," Peart's drums take command in "Danforth and Pape," and the Waltz of the Shreves staggers through triple time like something half-remembered. The whole thing was tracked essentially live at Rockfield Studios — Lee said they divided it into four parts and edited the tape together, but the feel is a single continuous performance. Up to forty takes to get it right. They spent more time recording this one track than their entire second album. You can hear that commitment in every transition — nothing is faked, nothing is programmed, nothing is safe.

## Emotional center

The feeling of being carried through something vast by people who know exactly where they're going — even when you don't.

## One clean sentence

The sound of three people dreaming the same dream at the same time and never once losing each other in it.
