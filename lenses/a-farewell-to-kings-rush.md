# A Farewell to Kings — Rush

**Track 1 / 5:51 / A Farewell to Kings, 1977**

---

## What this song feels like

A door opening into a courtyard from another century. The classical guitar starts alone — formal, careful, almost ceremonial — and for the first minute the world it builds is medieval, pastoral, untouched. Birdsong from outside the studio walls in Wales seeps into the recording. Then the modern world crashes in. Electric guitar, bass, drums — a violent shift from one age to another. The feeling isn't one thing or the other. It's the friction between them.

## Core emotional movement

**Stillness → Break → Build → Hold**

The opening minute is pure stillness — Lifeson's nylon-string guitar moving through 3/4 and 4/4 cycles, Lee's Minimoog and Peart's glockenspiel adding gentle weight, the whole texture quiet enough to hear the room. Then the break: electric guitar, bass, drums entering at full strength, dragging the song out of the courtyard and into the city. The build kicks in through 7/4 verses, racing forward, Peart's drums double-timing under the riff. The chorus holds at full intensity, then opens into a soaring guitar solo before circling back to the verse. The song doesn't return to the opening's quiet. It can't. Once you've heard the modern world come in, the medieval one is gone.

## What it's really about (emotionally)

The state of looking at something old and beautiful and knowing it can't come back. Not nostalgia — nostalgia softens things. This is sharper. The opening minute is a deliberate vision of an idealized past, and the shift to electric is the present arriving uninvited. The emotional center isn't grief for the past or rage at the present. It's the friction of holding both at once. Knowing the courtyard was always partly imagined. Knowing the city is real. And feeling the weight of having to live in the second one with the first one still ringing in your head.

## How the music mirrors it

The structural choice is the whole statement. Recording at Rockfield in Wales — a converted farmhouse studio surrounded by countryside — and letting the natural birdsong bleed into the track grounds the opening in a real, physical pastoral space. Lifeson's classical guitar plays in shifting time signatures (3/4 to 4/4 to 5/4) that feel free and unhurried, the way medieval lute music breathes. The Minimoog and glockenspiel are warm, ancient-sounding choices, not synth-as-modernity. Then the electric instruments arrive and the time signature locks into 7/4 — odd, driving, propulsive. The harmonic language shifts from modal pastoral to power chords. Lee's voice is high and full of passion, projected hard. Peart's drumming is double-time fusion underneath, restless, urban. The contrast isn't subtle and isn't meant to be. The "Cities full of hatred, fear and lies" section is the song stating its hand — but the music had already said it three minutes earlier, the moment the electric guitar hit. The closing verse asks for something "closer to the heart," which becomes the next song's whole answer. The title track raises the problem. The album is the response.

## Emotional center

The weight of standing in two ages at once and feeling the gap between them.

## One clean sentence

The sound of an old world fading while the new one crashes in — and the listener caught in the doorway between them.
