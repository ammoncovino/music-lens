# The Music Lens

The Music Lens describes what a song does emotionally inside the listener. It doesn't explain meaning, judge content, or prescribe action. It describes feeling, tone, posture, and internal movement.

Music is treated as a state, not a message.

---

## How It Works

The Music Lens is a framework — a set of rules for describing what a song does emotionally inside the listener. Not what the lyrics mean. Not what the artist intended. Not whether the song is good or bad. Just: what does it feel like, how does that feeling move, and where does it sit when the song is done.

I built the framework — the categories, the vocabulary, the principles, the rules. I feed it to an AI as a strict set of operating instructions. When I give it a song, it follows those rules. It doesn't interpret. It doesn't judge. It describes the internal experience.

The AI doesn't feel the music. But it holds the lens steady.

I built the lens. The AI holds it in place.

---

## Standard Output

Every song gets the same six-part read:

1. **What this song feels like** — the immediate emotional atmosphere
2. **Core emotional movement** — how the feeling changes over time
3. **What it's really about (emotionally)** — the underlying emotional state being expressed, not the narrative
4. **How the music mirrors it** — how the sound reflects the emotion (tempo, space, repetition, density, lift)
5. **Emotional center** — the stable emotional truth of the piece
6. **One clean sentence** — the entire emotional state, compressed

---

## Core Principles

1. Stay inside the listener's experience
2. Describe, do not interpret or evaluate
3. No external systems, politics, or frameworks
4. No moral conclusions
5. No advice or behavioral direction
6. Keep language human, simple, and grounded
7. Focus on emotional movement over lyrical meaning
8. Music is treated as a state, not a message

## Emotional Movements

These describe how a feeling behaves inside a song:

| Movement | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Stillness** | No forward push, suspended state |
| **Build** | Gradual layering or growth |
| **Drift** | Movement without direction |
| **Loop** | Repeating emotional cycle |
| **Hold** | Sustained tension without release |
| **Break** | Sudden release or collapse |
| **Return** | Inward movement to center |
| **Rise** | Upward expansion or release |

## Tone Types

| Tone | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Heavy** | Weight, gravity |
| **Light** | Openness, ease |
| **Tense** | Pressure, compression |
| **Calm** | Stability, grounding |
| **Expansive** | Widening space |
| **Detached** | Emotional distance |
| **Warm** | Connection, softness |
| **Cold** | Controlled, reduced emotion |

---

## Secondary States

These sit outside the main arc. A song can live entirely inside one:

- **Looping** — stuck in tension (addiction, longing, repetition)
- **Impulse** — raw energy without reflection
- **Boundary** — rejection, refusal, distancing
- **Pressure** — buildup without release
- **Posture** — stance or identity expression
- **Presence** — no movement, just being
- **Awe** — encountering something larger than self

---

## The Skeleton (Optional Reference Arc)

Some songs follow a natural emotional progression:

**Sit → Make → Keep going → Notice what's gone → Re-center → Rise**

1. **Alone** — solitude, vastness, presence without connection
2. **Build** — intentional, gentle, creation of structure
3. **Continue** — steady forward motion, endurance
4. **Acknowledge Loss** — heavy, quiet, acceptance of absence
5. **Re-center** — calm, grounded, inward return
6. **Rise** — expansive, elevated, transcendence

Not every song follows this. Many don't. It's a reference point, not a requirement.

---

## Rules of Operation

- Do not explain lyrics literally
- Do not analyze artist intent
- Do not connect to politics, systems, or society
- Do not prescribe meaning
- Do not resolve the listener's state
- Do not moralize behavior or themes
- Do not give advice unless explicitly asked
